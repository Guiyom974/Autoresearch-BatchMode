### Summary of Concepts
**Chebyshev's Bias and Prime Races:** 
First observed by Pafnuty Chebyshev in 1853, "Chebyshev's bias" refers to the phenomenon where prime numbers tend to be unevenly distributed among different residue classes modulo $q$ [[1]](https://msp.org/ant/2014/8-7/ant-v8-n7-p.pdf). For example, primes congruent to 3 mod 4 appear more frequently than those congruent to 1 mod 4. The comparative study of these distributions is known as "prime number races" or the Shanks–Rényi prime number race [[2]](https://umontreal.scholaris.ca/bitstreams/59d95930-05e9-4988-a001-d7ed23b0c9e1/download).

**The Rubinstein-Sarnak Framework:**
In 1994, Michael Rubinstein and Peter Sarnak published a highly influential paper that provided a rigorous mathematical framework for understanding Chebyshev's bias [[3]](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1909.03975)[[4]](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/322843741_Prime_Number_Races). They proved conditionally that these prime number races are "inclusive" and established the existence of a limiting logarithmic distribution for the races [[4]](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/322843741_Prime_Number_Races). 

**Dirichlet Characters and L-functions:**
The Rubinstein-Sarnak approach relies heavily on the analytic properties of Dirichlet $L$-functions, which are constructed using Dirichlet characters [[4]](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/322843741_Prime_Number_Races). The bias in prime races is deeply connected to the low-lying zeros of these $L$-functions [[1]](https://msp.org/ant/2014/8-7/ant-v8-n7-p.pdf). Rubinstein and Sarnak's results were conditional on two major assumptions:
1. **The Generalized Riemann Hypothesis (GRH)** for Dirichlet $L$-functions.
2. **The Linear Independence (LI) hypothesis**, which assumes that the imaginary parts of the non-trivial zeros of Dirichlet $L$-functions are linearly independent over the rational numbers [[2]](https://umontreal.scholaris.ca/bitstreams/59d95930-05e9-4988-a001-d7ed23b0c9e1/download)[[5]](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/236578549_Elliptic_Curves_of_Unbounded_Rank_and_Chebyshev's_Bias). 

Recent research has focused on generalizing these prime races to function fields, elliptic curves, and multiple residue classes, often by weakening the strict Linear Independence hypothesis required by Rubinstein and Sarnak [[3]](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1909.03975)[[4]](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/322843741_Prime_Number_Races).

### Query: "quadratic residue" "non-residue" prime count modulo 210 OR 2310

### 1. Quadratic Reciprocity and Residue Patterns
A number modulo an odd prime $p$ is a **quadratic residue** if it is congruent to a perfect square modulo $p$; otherwise, it is a **quadratic non-residue**. A fundamental property of the multiplicative group of a finite field of order $p$ is that there is always an exactly equal number of quadratic residues and non-residues (excluding zero). The product of two residues or two non-residues results in a residue, while the product of a residue and a non-residue results in a non-residue [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_reciprocity).

### 2. Bounds for Prime Character Non-residues
Research by Paul Pollack explores the bounds and distribution of the first several prime character non-residues. The study utilizes character sums, Dickman's function, and the Burgess–Norton upper bound to estimate the count of smooth numbers and the smallest prime non-residues modulo $p$. Since the Legendre symbol is completely multiplicative, the smallest non-residue modulo a prime is always guaranteed to be a prime itself [[2]](https://pollack.uga.edu/hudson.pdf).

### 3. Distribution Properties and Counting Functions
Recent papers (e.g., published in MDPI and Semantic Scholar) study the computational problems involving the symmetry sums of quadratic residues and non-residues modulo $p$. By using the Legendre symbol and character sum estimates, mathematicians have developed exact calculation formulas and asymptotic formulas for specific counting functions. This has helped solve conjectures regarding the existence of integers $a$ where $a$, $a + \bar{a}$, and $a - \bar{a}$ are all quadratic residues or non-residues modulo $p$ [[3]](https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/7f2c/cc244574248601fda7de9dace4d4f83d8ed0.pdf)[[4]](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/8/8/1337).

### 4. Factorization and Sieving Modulo 210
In computational number theory and factorization algorithms, primorials like **210** ($2 \times 3 \times 5 \times 7$) are frequently used as moduli to restrict the search space for prime factors. Because a perfect square can only take on a specific subset of possible residues modulo 210 (e.g., 1, 4, 9, 15, 16, etc.), checking if a number is a quadratic residue modulo 210 acts as a highly efficient filter/sieve to eliminate non-squares and composite candidates during large integer factorization [[5]](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/2592401/if-n-is-a-quadratic-residue-modulo-p-for-all-primes-pn-is-n-a-perfect-square)[[6]](https://projecteuclid.org/journals/bulletin-of-the-american-mathematical-society/volume-30/issue-9-10/Methods-for-finding-factors-of-large-integers/bams/1183486232.pdf).

### 5. Electronic Number Sieves Modulo 2310
Similar to 210, the next primorial **2310** ($2 \times 3 \times 5 \times 7 \times 11$) is heavily utilized in prime sieving algorithms. Historical and modern electronic number sieves use the concept of "apparent residue character" modulo 2310 to rapidly filter out non-primes and find twin primes. By calculating quadratic residues modulo 2310, hardware sieves can bypass millions of composite numbers per second, drastically reducing the computational load required for primality testing [[7]](https://mspace.lib.umanitoba.ca/bitstream/1993/12210/1/Lukes_A_very.pdf)[[8]](https://www.quora.com/Is-it-true-that-there-is-an-infinity-of-primes-p-such-that-the-interval-p-30p-2-does-not-contain-primes-of-the-form-kp-1-Whats-the-smallest-example).

### 1. Cramér's Probabilistic Model
Cramér's model is a foundational probabilistic model used to predict the behavior and distribution of prime numbers [[3]](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/680122/cram%C3%A9rs-model-the-prime-numbers-and-their-distribution-part-1). It models the sequence of primes by assuming that the "probability" of a number $n$ being prime is $1/\ln(n)$, which aligns with the Prime Number Theorem [[3]](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/680122/cram%C3%A9rs-model-the-prime-numbers-and-their-distribution-part-1). Computational validations of this model often involve checking properties almost surely for the sequence and comparing them against the actual sequence of primes [[3]](https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/680122/cram%C3%A9rs-model-the-prime-numbers-and-their-distribution-part-1).

### 2. Tweedie Exponential Dispersion Models (Scale Invariant Distribution)
A computational analysis of the deviations between the actual positions of prime numbers and their predicted positions (using Riemann's counting formula) shows that these deviations can be described by Tweedie exponential dispersion models [[4]](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-3197/3/4/528). 
* **Computational Validation Scale:** The variance of these deviations follows a power-law relationship with the mean (known as Taylor's power law or fluctuation scaling) [[4]](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-3197/3/4/528). This demonstrates a scale-invariant, fractal-like pattern in the distribution of primes, validated computationally across sequential enumerative bins [[4]](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-3197/3/4/528).

### 3. Combinatorial Models of Prime Distribution
Recent research proposes combinatorial models (such as "First Occurrence Sequences" or FOS) that reproduce the stochastic structure of the prime sequence [[5]](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/9/11/1224). 
* **Validation:** These models are validated by deriving the probability distribution of the combinatorial counterpart of the $n$-th prime number and estimating the prime-counting function $\pi(x)$ [[5]](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/9/11/1224). They have been used to computationally predict the number of consecutive prime pairs as a function of the gap $d$, matching empirical data for large gaps [[5]](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/9/11/1224).
