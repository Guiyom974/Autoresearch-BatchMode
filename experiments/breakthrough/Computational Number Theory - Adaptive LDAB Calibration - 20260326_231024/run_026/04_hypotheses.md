**Hypotheses for the 1.17‑scaling problem**  
Below are four **testable** hypotheses that (i) extend the insight that the variance‑to‑mean ratio of primorial gaps grows like \((\log P_k)^{\beta}\) with \(\beta\approx1.17\), (ii) explain why the earlier 0.56‑conjecture was wrong, (iii) predict the true asymptotic behaviour, and (iv) guarantee that the required high‑precision calculations can actually be performed for \(k\ge 9\).  Each hypothesis is accompanied by a short rationale that shows why it can be falsified and by a concrete experimental plan.

---

### 1.  **The exponent \(\beta(k)\) drifts toward 1 for large \(k\).**  

**Statement**  
The apparent constant \(\beta\approx1.17\) is a **finite‑size effect**.  Inclusion‑exclusion corrections generate a term of order \(1/\log P_k\) so that  

\[
\beta(k)=\underbrace{1}_{\text{asymptotic slope}}
          \;+\;\frac{c}{\log P_k}+o\!\left(\frac{1}{\log P_k}\right),
\qquad c>0 .
\]

Consequently \(\beta(k)\) will be measurable‑ly larger than 1 for modest \(k\) but will converge **downward** to 1 as \(k\to\infty\).

**Why it is testable**  
If the relationship above holds, the sequence \(\beta(k)\) estimated from data for successive primorials will show a systematic decline.  The decline can be quantified with a confidence interval and tested against the null hypothesis \(\beta(k)=\beta_0\) (constant).

**Experiment to test it**  

1. **Exact computation** – Use the generator‑based, \(O(1)\)-memory sieve to obtain the full gap list for the 9‑th, 10‑th, 11‑th and (if resources allow) 12‑th primorials.  Compute the sample mean \(\mu_k\) and variance \(\sigma^2_k\) with arbitrary‑precision arithmetic (≥128‑bit integers, later converted to high‑precision floats).  
2. **Window‑based exponent estimation** – For each \(k\) fit the model  

   \[
   \log R(k)=\log A+\beta(k)\,\log\!\bigl(\log P_k\bigr)
   \]

   using ordinary least‑squares on the five data points \((k,\log R(k),\log\!\log P_k)\).  Allow \(\beta(k)\) to vary with a quadratic correction in \(1/\log P_k\).  
3. **Statistical test** – Perform a two‑sided t‑test (or AIC‑based model selection) of the null \(\beta(k)=1.17\) against the alternative \(\beta(k)=1+c/\log P_k\).  Rejection of the constant‑\(\beta\) model (p < 0.05) for \(k\ge 9\) would confirm the drift.

---

### 2.  **A sub‑leading inclusion‑exclusion correction produces a measurable curvature in the log‑log plot.**  

**Statement**  
Beyond the leading power‑law, the true functional form is  

\[
R(k)=A\;\bigl(\log P_k\bigr)^{\beta}\;
      \Bigl[1+\frac{\alpha}{\log P_k}+O\!\bigl((\log P_k)^{-2}\bigr)\Bigr],
\]

where the coefficient \(\alpha\) originates from **second‑order inclusion‑exclusion terms** (pairwise correlations among gaps).  This predicts a systematic concave deviation from a pure straight line when \(\log R\) is plotted against \(\log\!\log P_k\).

**Why it is testable**  
If \(\alpha\neq0\), a quadratic term \(\gamma\,(\log\!\log P_k)^{-1}\) will improve the fit significantly (increase in \(R^{2}\) and a statistically significant t‑statistic for \(\gamma\)).  Conversely, a pure power‑law will show random residuals.

**Experiment to test it**  

1. **Fit a hierarchy of models** to the extended data set \(\{k=3,\dots,12\}\):  

   *Model 0*: \(\log R = \log A + \beta\,\log\!\log P_k\).  
   *Model 1*: \(\log R = \log A + \beta\,\log\!\log P_k + \alpha\,(\log\!\log P_k)^{-1}\).  

   Use non‑linear regression (Levenberg–Marquardt) with error propagation from the high‑precision variance estimates.  
2. **Model comparison** – Compute the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) for each model.  A ΔBIC > 6 (strong evidence) in favour of Model 1 would indicate the presence of the \(\alpha\) term.  
3. **Interpretation** – If the \(\alpha\) term is significant, relate it analytically to the sum  

   \[
   \alpha = \sum_{1\le i<j\le k}\frac{\log p_i\log p_j}{p_i p_j},
   \]

   which can be evaluated independently and compared to the empirical estimate.

### 3.  **The 0.56‑conjecture failed because it ignored inter‑gap covariance generated by inclusion‑exclusion.**  

**Statement**  
The earlier derivation assumed that gaps in the set \(\{1\le n\le P_k:\gcd(n,P_k)=1\}\) are **independent**, leading to a variance scaling proportional to \((\log P_k)^{0.56}\).  In reality the inclusion‑exclusion structure induces a non‑zero covariance  

\[
\mathrm{Cov}(\Delta_i,\Delta_j)=\sum_{r=1}^{k}\frac{(-1)^{r}}{p_{i_1}\cdots p_{i_r}}\,
                                   \bigl[\text{some analytic function of }r\bigr],
\]

which contributes a term scaling as \(\log\log P_k\) to the variance.  When this covariance is incorporated, the exponent jumps to \(\approx1.17\).

**Why it is testable**  
If we explicitly construct a **reduced model** that retains only the first‑order inclusion‑exclusion probabilities (i.e., neglects all pairwise and higher‑order covariances) and simulate gaps under that model, its predicted exponent will be ≈0.56.  The full model (including covariances) will reproduce ≈1.17.  A direct comparison therefore discriminates between the two hypotheses.

**Experiment to test it**  

1. **Monte‑Carlo simulation** –  
   * (a) *Naïve model*: Generate a surrogate coprime sequence by treating each integer as “coprime” with probability \(\prod_{i=1}^{k}(1-1/p_i)\) independently.  Compute \(R_{\text{naïve}}(k)\).  
   * (b) *Covariance‑aware model*: Use the exact inclusion‑exclusion probabilities to draw gaps from a multivariate normal with the computed covariance matrix (dimension equal to the number of distinct gap lengths).  Compute \(R_{\text{cov}}(k)\).  
2. **Repeat** for \(k=5,\dots,10\) (the range where the 0.56 result was originally reported).  
3. **Statistical comparison** – Plot both series on the same log‑log axes.  A crossing of the naïve series below the observed data (≈1.17) and a close match of the covariance‑aware series to the empirical points would confirm the hypothesis.  Use a paired t‑test on the log‑residuals to quantify significance.

### 4.  **Exact computation of \(R(k)\) up to \(k=12\) is feasible within the allocated HPC budget using an \(O(1)\)-memory streaming algorithm.**  

**Statement**  
A generator‑based sieve that (i) never stores the full array of length \(P_k\), (ii) processes the integers in blocks that fit into L2/L3 cache, and (iii) aggregates gap counts in a fixed‑size hash map can evaluate \(\mu_k\) and \(\sigma^2_k\) for the 12‑th primorial (\(P_{12}=6,469,693,230\times 13\approx 8.4\times10^{10}\)) in **≤ 10⁵ CPU‑hours** with a **maximum resident memory ≤ 256 MiB** and a **relative numerical error < 10⁻⁹**.

**Why it is testable**  
If the algorithm’s time‑complexity and memory usage match the theoretical bounds, we can actually run it on modern HPC hardware (e.g., a 1 000‑node cluster with 64 cores each) and observe the predicted runtime and precision.  Failure to meet the bounds would falsify the statement.

**Experiment to test it**  

1. **Implementation** – Write the generator in C++ (or Rust) using the **bitset‑free segmented sieve** described in the methodology.  The only persistent state is a small array of counters (one per possible gap length up to the maximum observed gap for \(k=11\), ≈ 1 000 000).  
2. **Benchmarking** – Run the program on a single node for \(k=9\) and \(k=10\) to obtain baseline scaling (CPU‑seconds vs. \(P_k\)).  Extrapolate to \(k=12\).  
3. **Full‑scale run** – Submit the extrapolated job to a HPC queue; monitor wall‑clock time, CPU‑time, peak RAM, and I/O volume.  After completion, compute \(R(12)\) with both 64‑bit and 128‑bit integer accumulation, then convert to IEEE‑754 double and arbitrary‑precision decimal for error analysis.  
4. **Verification** – Compare the resulting \(R(12)\) to the prediction from Hypothesis 1 (the extrapolated \(\beta(k)\)).  If the measured value lies within the predicted 95 % confidence interval, the feasibility claim is validated.

### 5.  **A closed‑form Euler‑product expression approximates \(R(k)\) to within 0.1 % for all \(k\ge 5\).** *(Optional fifth hypothesis – include if a fifth is desired.)*  

**Statement**  
The variance‑to‑mean ratio can be written as  

\[
R(k) \;=\; \exp\!\Bigl(\gamma\,\sigma_k\;+\;\delta\Bigr)
          \quad\text{with}\quad
\sigma_k:=\sum_{i=1}^{k}\frac{1}{p_i},
\]

where \(\gamma\approx1.17\) (the observed exponent) and \(\delta\) is a small constant (≈ –0.2).  This form follows from writing the probability that two randomly chosen integers are both coprime to \(P_k\) as an Euler product and expanding the resulting logarithm.

**Why it is testable**  
If the formula holds, then plotting \(\log R(k)\) versus \(\sigma_k\) should be linear with slope \(\gamma\) and intercept \(\delta\).  The fit should have \(R^{2}>0.999\) and residuals < 0.001.

**Experiment**  

1. Compute \(\sigma_k\) for \(k=3,\dots,12\) exactly (high‑precision rational arithmetic).  
2. Regress \(\log R(k)\) on \(\sigma_k\) using ordinary linear regression; extract \(\hat\gamma\) and \(\hat\delta\).  
3. Test the predictive power by using the fitted model to **forecast** \(R(12)\) and compare with the measured value from the HPC run (Hypothesis 4).  A relative error < 0.1 % confirms the Euler‑product hypothesis.
