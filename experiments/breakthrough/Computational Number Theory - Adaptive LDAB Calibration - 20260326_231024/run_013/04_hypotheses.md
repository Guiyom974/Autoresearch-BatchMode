**Hypotheses (5) – each is stated, why it can be tested, and what experiment would examine it.  
All of them build on the earlier artifact‑free dataset (k = 3 → 7) that showed a steady rise of the variance‑to‑mean² ratio (R ≈ 0.17 → 0.33) and on the finding that floating‑point underflow must be avoided when the primorial becomes large.**

### 1.  **Quadratic‑log correction outperforms the simple linear‑log model**

**Statement**  
\(R(k)=\frac13-\frac{C_{1}}{\log P_{k}}+\frac{C_{2}}{\log^{2}P_{k}}\) (Model A) fitted to the exact values for \(k=3,\dots,8\) will give an **\(R^{2}\ge0.95\)** and a **significantly smaller residual standard error** than the old \(R(k)=\frac13-\frac{C}{\log P_{k}}\).

**Why it is testable**  
We have precise, artifact‑free values of \(R(k)\) for \(k=3\ldots8\) and a well‑defined goodness‑of‑fit metric (coefficient of determination).  The two extra parameters \(C_{1},C_{2}\) can be estimated by ordinary least squares, and the improvement over the one‑parameter model can be assessed with an F‑test or AIC comparison.

**Experiment**  
1. **Exact enumeration** – implement a segmented, memory‑efficient sieve for the primorial \(P_{8}=9699690\) (or reuse the already‑computed exact gap list).  Compute the mean and variance of the reduced‑residue gaps and obtain \(R(k)\) for each \(k\).  
2. **Fit both models** – use non‑linear regression (or linear regression on the transformed variables) to obtain the best‑fit coefficients for Model A and for the simple model.  
3. **Compare** – calculate \(R^{2}\), residual standard error, and run an F‑test (extra sum‑of‑squares) to decide whether the quadratic term adds statistically significant explanatory power.

---

### 2.  **The true asymptotic limit of \(R(k)\) exceeds \(1/3\)**  

**Statement**  
When we let the asymptote be a free parameter (Model B: \(R(k)=A-\frac{B}{\log P_{k}}\)), the fitted value of \(A\) will be **larger than \(1/3\)** and significantly different from it (p < 0.05).  Consequently, the variance‑to‑mean² ratio does not converge downward to \(1/3\) but to a higher ceiling.

**Why it is testable**  
Model B is a direct extension of the original hypothesis where the “limit” was fixed at \(1/3\).  By estimating \(A\) we can perform a t‑test (or a confidence‑interval check) against the null hypothesis \(A=1/3\).  The data set for \(k=3\ldots8\) already shows a monotonic increase toward the region around 0.33–0.36, providing enough information to test the hypothesis.

**Experiment**  
1. **Fit Model B** to the same exact \(R(k)\) data using weighted least squares (weights can be taken as the inverse of the estimated variance of each \(R(k)\), which is obtainable from the exact counts).  
2. **Construct a 95 % confidence interval for \(A\)**.  If the interval lies entirely above \(1/3\), reject the null.  
3. **Sanity check** – also verify that the fitted \(B>0\) (ensuring the correction term has the right sign).

### 3.  **A power‑law correction predicts the out‑of‑sample \(R(9)\) value more accurately than any log‑correction model**  

**Statement**  
\(R(k)=A-B\,P_{k}^{-\alpha}\) (Model C) will produce a **prediction for \(R(9)\)** that falls **within a 5 % prediction interval** of the empirical value, whereas the log‑correction models (A and B) will miss this target.

**Why it is testable**  
Prediction‑interval coverage is a standard out‑of‑sample diagnostic.  Once the exact value for \(k=9\) is known we can compare the actual \(R(9)\) to the intervals generated by each fitted model.  The hypothesis is falsifiable: if the observed \(R(9)\) lies outside the 5 % band of Model C, the hypothesis is refuted.

**Experiment**  
1. **Calibrate Models A, B, C** on the \(k=3\ldots8\) dataset, obtaining coefficient estimates and their covariance matrices.  
2. **Compute the 5 % prediction interval** for each model at \(P_{9}=223\,092\,870\).  For Model C this involves the usual formula \(\hat{R}\pm t_{0.975,n-p}\, \sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^{2}+ \mathbf{x}_{9}^{\!T} \operatorname{Cov}(\hat{\beta})\mathbf{x}_{9}}\).  
3. **Run the exact enumeration** for \(k=9\) (segmented sieve with arbitrary‑precision arithmetic to avoid underflow).  Compute the empirical \(R(9)\).  
4. **Check coverage** – record which models contain the empirical \(R(9)\) inside their interval.  The model with the smallest absolute error and the correct coverage is the one that best satisfies the hypothesis.

### 4.  **The second‑order logarithmic term is mathematically motivated by secondary sieving effects and its coefficient \(C_{2}\) is statistically non‑zero**  

**Statement**  
In Model A the coefficient \(C_{2}\) will be **positive and significantly different from zero (p < 0.05)**.  This supports the theoretical claim that the quadratic term captures the contribution of “second‑order” coprimality constraints that arise when the reduced residue system is sieved by the product of the first \(k\) primes.

**Why it is testable**  
Statistical significance of \(C_{2}\) can be evaluated directly from the regression output (t‑statistic).  If the p‑value is below the chosen threshold, the null hypothesis \(C_{2}=0\) is rejected, confirming that the higher‑order term is needed.

**Experiment**  
1. **Regress Model A** as in Hypothesis 1, extract the estimate \(\hat{C}_{2}\) and its standard error.  
2. **Compute the t‑statistic** \(t=\hat{C}_{2}/\mathrm{SE}(\hat{C}_{2})\) and compare to the critical value for the appropriate degrees of freedom (here \(n-p = 8-3 = 5\)).  
3. **Interpret** – a positive, significant \(C_{2}\) would be consistent with the idea that the variance reduction due to the primorial’s sieving is not fully captured by a single‑log correction, but requires an additional \(\log^{-2}\) term reflecting the way secondary prime constraints interact.

### 5.  **\(R(k)\) is bounded above by a theoretical ceiling \(C_{\max}<0.5\) and the observed sequence for \(k\le9\) approaches this bound**  

**Statement**  
Empirical \(R(k)\) values (for \(k=3,\dots,9\)) will lie **strictly below 0.5** and the fitted models (especially Model C) will suggest an asymptote \(A<C_{\max}=0.5\).  Moreover, the **difference \(C_{\max}-R(k)\)** will decrease monotonically, indicating convergence.

**Why it is testable**  
The bound is a concrete, falsifiable inequality: any computed \(R(k)\) exceeding 0.5 would instantly disprove the hypothesis.  The convergence claim can be examined by looking at the **monotonicity of the residuals** from any of the calibrated models.

**Experiment**  
1. **Exact enumeration** for \(k=9\) (as described above) provides the largest known \(R(k)\).  
2. **Check inequality** – verify \(R(9) < 0.5\).  
3. **Fit a simple asymptotic model** (e.g., Model C with \(A\) as the asymptote) and compute the 95 % confidence interval for \(A\).  If the upper confidence bound is still below 0.5, the hypothesis holds.  
4. **Plot the convergence** – plot \(R(k)\) versus \(1/\log P_{k}\) or \(P_{k}^{-\alpha}\) and illustrate that the gap to the asymptote shrinks, confirming the bound’s tightness.
