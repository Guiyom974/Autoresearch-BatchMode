**Hypotheses – Resolving the VMR collapse at k ≥ 8**

| # | Hypothesis (statement) | Why it is testable | How we would test it (experiment) |
|---|--------------------------|--------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **1** | **The VMR collapse at k = 8 is caused by an overflow (or catastrophic loss of precision) that occurs in the *sum‑of‑squared‑gaps* accumulation, not in the mean or the gap‑count computation.** | The overflow can be observed directly by comparing the same intermediate quantities computed with 64‑bit integers/doubles versus arbitrary‑precision integers. If the two calculations differ, the hypothesis is confirmed; if they agree, the overflow must be elsewhere. | 1. Write a diagnostic version of the pipeline that logs every intermediate sum (∑ g, ∑ g²) and the final variance after each iteration for k = 8. <br>2. The 20 % tolerance is arbitrary but chosen to be well above numerical noise. | 1. Fit a model (e.g., VMR(k) = A·k^γ) to the artifact‑free VMRs for k = 1…7 using `scipy.optimize.curve_fit`. <br>2. | Prior finding | Link to new hypotheses |

| **“Variance calculations become inconsistent for small k; higher‑precision arithmetic is needed”** | Hypothesis 1 isolates *where* the inconsistency (overflow) actually occurs. |
| **“A methodological artifact was identified and corrected, giving artifact‑free gap variances for k ≤ 7”** | Hypothesis 2 tests whether the same correction strategy (higher precision) also fixes the later k values. |
| **“No clear scaling emerged because of numerical problems”** | Hypothesis 3 checks whether the corrected data now follow a clean trajectory, and Hypothesis 4 evaluates whether the scaling exponent γ finally converges to the theoretically conjectured ≈ 0.633. |

Together, the five hypotheses provide a **logical, falsifiable roadmap**: (i) locate the overflow, (ii) fix it with arbitrary precision, (iii) verify monotonicity, (iv) confirm trajectory continuity and the true scaling exponent, and (v) demonstrate that the fix is computationally practical. Each hypothesis can be refuted by a single well‑designed computation, satisfying the requirement for testability.
