CONCLUSIONS
================================================================================

1. HYPOTHESIS 1 (Boundary conditions):
   Boundary conditions influence stability, but NaNs are avoidable with
   appropriate thresholds. The model transitions smoothly between regimes.

2. HYPOTHESIS 2 (Time step):
   Small time steps (dt ≤ 0.5 yr) produce stable power-law fits (R² > 0.5)
   while larger steps cause numerical instability and NaN parameters.

3. HYPOTHESIS 3 (Cloud parameterization):
   Cloud feedback can be net cooling (negative forcing) or net warming
   depending on optical depth and sensitivity. High optical depth + low
   sensitivity → cooling; low optical depth + high sensitivity → warming.

4. HYPOTHESIS 4 (Dominant parameters):
   Solar multiplier contributes more variance in temperature than albedo
   parameters, supporting the finding that insolation is a primary driver.

5. HYPOTHESIS 5 (Bistability):
   Bistability was NOT observed in the coupled system under current
   parameterization, suggesting the model lacks strong ice-albedo
   hysteresis. This may require sharper transition functions or higher
   heat capacity contrasts.

OVERALL:
   The coupled system is numerically sensitive to time step and boundary
   conditions. To obtain reliable power-law exponents, use dt ≤ 0.5 years
   and moderate temperature thresholds. Cloud feedbacks are highly
   parameterization-dependent and require careful calibration against
   observations to avoid spurious feedback strengths.
