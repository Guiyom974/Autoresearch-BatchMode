A sensitivity analysis of a 1D Energy Balance Model (EBM) focusing on ice-albedo and cloud feedbacks requires examining how variations in model parameters and assumptions affect climate sensitivity and warming predictions. This involves investigating the impact of boundary conditions, time steps, and specific cloud parameterizations to identify stable parameter regimes for statistically significant results [[1]](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/377789737_Assessing_Climate_Sensitivity_and_Mechanisms_in_a_One-Dimensional_Climate_Energy_Balance_Model)[[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3003081/). Research has shown that land albedo, solar multiplier, and coefficients in the Stefan-Boltzmann Law are significant contributors to climate dynamics in EBMs, with ice-albedo feedback playing a notable role in phenomena like "Snowball Earth" scenarios [[1]](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/377789737_Assessing_Climate_Sensitivity_and_Mechanisms_in_a_One-Dimensional_Climate_Energy_Balance_Model). Furthermore, the interaction between cloud feedbacks and surface albedo is crucial, as clouds can mask albedo changes and influence the overall climate sensitivity, with significant model-to-model variability in cloud feedback representation [[3]](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/23579946_An_energy_balance_climate_model_with_cloud_feedbacks)[[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/earth-science/articles/10.3389/feart.2021.769844/full).
To address the undefined power-law fits and non-significant warming reductions, a systematic evaluation of EBM components is necessary. This includes exploring how different parameterizations of cloud properties (e.g., fixed cloud temperature vs. fixed cloud altitude) influence global sensitivity [[5]](https://www.giss.nasa.gov/pubs/abs/wa03100m.html). Studies have also highlighted the importance of considering the latitudinal dependence of processes like ice feedback, which is better represented in a 1D EBM than a 0D model [[6]](https://courses.ems.psu.edu/meteo469/node/212)[[7]](https://www.gfy.ku.dk/~kaas/onedmodel/Energybalancemodel.pdf). The sensitivity of climate models to various parameters, including those related to cloud and surface albedo, is a critical area of research, with ongoing efforts to improve their representation and reduce uncertainty in climate projections [[4]](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/earth-science/articles/10.3389/feart.2021.769844/full)[[8]](https://www.atmos.albany.edu/facstaff/brose/classes/ATM623_Spring2015/Notes/Lectures/Lecture03%20--%20Climate%20sensitivity%20and%20feedback.html).
The problem of undefined power-law fits and non-significant results suggests that the model's behavior is highly sensitive to its underlying assumptions and parameter choices [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3003081/)[[8]](https://www.atmos.albany.edu/facstaff/brose/classes/ATM623_Spring2015/Notes/Lectures/Lecture03%20--%20Climate%20sensitivity%20and%20feedback.html). Therefore, a comprehensive sensitivity analysis should focus on quantifying the impact of these assumptions. This includes investigating how changes in boundary conditions, such as solar constant or atmospheric composition, and temporal discretization (time steps) affect model stability and feedback mechanisms [[7]](https://www.gfy.ku.dk/~kaas/onedmodel/Energybalancemodel.pdf)[[8]](https://www.atmos.albany.edu/facstaff/brose/classes/ATM623_Spring2015/Notes/Lectures/Lecture03%20--%20Climate%20sensitivity%20and%20feedback.html).
